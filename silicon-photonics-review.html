Everyone's fighting over which AI chip stock to buy — NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom. But here's what they're missing: the GPUs aren't the bottleneck anymore. The bottleneck is the cable connecting them.

We already know power is a bottleneck — I've covered that. Nuclear, natural gas, the whole energy story. But there's a second bottleneck that almost nobody is talking about. Right now, AI data centers use copper wires to move data between thousands of GPUs. And at 1.6 terabits per second — the speed required for next-gen AI training — copper generates so much heat that it literally can't go any faster. The industry has one option: replace copper with light. It's called silicon photonics, and NVIDIA just went all-in — announcing co-packaged optical switches and a direct partnership with a specialty chipmaker that most investors have never heard of. This is the plumbing play for AI.


So in this video, I'm going to break down what silicon photonics actually is — in plain English. I'll cover why NVIDIA just went all-in on this technology. And then I'm going to walk you through the entire silicon photonics supply chain — from the foundries that manufacture the chips, to the companies building the optical components, all the way up to the architects designing the systems. Every company worth looking at in this space. I am not a financial advisor. This is not financial advice. This is just my research and my perspective as an investor.

The Problem: Copper Is Dying


Here's what most people miss about AI infrastructure. Everyone talks about the GPUs — the processing power. And yeah, that matters. But training a model like GPT-5 requires thousands of GPUs working together simultaneously. They have to talk to each other constantly. And the network connecting those GPUs? That's now the bottleneck. Not the chips themselves.


[B-ROLL: simple animation — GPUs connected by wires, data flowing]


Right now, most data centers use copper cables to move data between chips. And copper works fine — up to a point. The industry is moving from 800 Gigabit connections to 1.6 Terabit connections. That's 1,600 Gigabits per second. For context, your home internet is probably 1 Gbps. We're talking 1,600 times faster than that.


And here's the problem. At those speeds, copper generates too much heat. It eats too much power. And it physically cannot keep up beyond about 3 meters of distance. So you're building these massive AI factories with millions of GPUs — and the wires connecting them are the weakest link.


It's like building a Formula 1 car and then connecting the engine to the wheels with a rubber band. At some point, the rubber band snaps.



The Solution: Light on a Chip


So what's the fix? Silicon photonics.


[B-ROLL: fiber optic light pulses, silicon wafer manufacturing]


And I know that sounds complicated. But the concept is actually pretty simple. Instead of pushing data through copper using electrical signals, you push it through using light — photons instead of electrons. And the clever part is you do it on a regular silicon chip. The same kind of chip used to make processors. Which means you can manufacture these in existing semiconductor fabs, making them way more scalable and cheaper than traditional fiber optics.


Here's why this matters right now. A traditional 1.6 Terabit transceiver — the device that converts data into optical signals — uses about 30 watts of power. More than half of that is just the digital signal processor. NVIDIA's co-packaged silicon photonics approach eliminates that DSP entirely. The result? 3.5 times lower power consumption.


[EDITOR: show NVIDIA technical blog graphic comparing power consumption — source: developer.nvidia.com]


And when you're running a data center that consumes as much electricity as a small city, 3.5x power savings is a massive deal. That's not incremental. That's transformational.


The market reflects this. Silicon photonics is roughly a $3 billion market today. And depending on which research firm you ask — Precedence Research, Mordor Intelligence, Fortune Business Insights — it's growing at 25 to 29% annually. Conservative estimates put it at $10 billion by 2030. The most aggressive estimates say nearly $29 billion by 2034.


[EDITOR: show market size comparison graphic — $3B today → $10-29B by 2030-2034]


And LightCounting — one of the leading optical industry analysts — published a report in November 2025 literally titled "2026: The Year of Silicon Photonics."



NVIDIA's Big Bet


Now here's where it gets really interesting for investors.


At GTC 2025 — NVIDIA's annual conference — Jensen Huang unveiled their Spectrum-X and Quantum-X networking switches. Both built with co-packaged silicon photonics. This was NVIDIA telling the entire industry: the future of AI networking runs on light, not copper.


[B-ROLL: Jensen Huang GTC 2025 keynote footage]


And then on February 5th — just 10 days ago — NVIDIA announced a direct partnership with Tower Semiconductor to build 1.6 Terabit optical modules designed specifically for NVIDIA networking protocols. Gilad Shainer, NVIDIA's SVP of Networking, said: "The exponential growth of AI is driving the need for a new class of high-speed, scalable networking."


[SCREENSHOT: Tower Semi + NVIDIA press release — towersemi.com]


And if you've followed NVIDIA for any amount of time, you know the pattern. When Jensen builds a technology into the NVIDIA stack, the entire supply chain follows. It happened with CUDA. It happened with InfiniBand. And now it's happening with silicon photonics.


So the question becomes — who benefits? And I want to be thorough here. I'm not just going to show you my top three picks and call it a day. I'm going to walk you through every company that has meaningful exposure to this theme. Because I think the best way to understand a technology like this is to follow the supply chain — layer by layer. Who manufactures the chips. Who builds the optical components. Who designs the systems. And who's trying to keep up.



How I'm Organizing This


Think of the silicon photonics ecosystem like a pyramid. At the base, you've got the foundries — the factories that actually manufacture these chips. One layer up, you've got the component makers — the companies building the lasers and transceivers. Then at the top, you've got the architects — the companies designing the networking systems that tie it all together. And off to the side, you've got the legacy players — big companies that are relevant to the conversation but coming at it from a different angle.


I'm going to walk you through each layer. And at the very end, I'll give you my personal ranking — strongest to weakest — so you can see exactly where my conviction sits.


Let's start at the foundation.



THE FOUNDRIES — Who Manufactures the Chips


Everything in silicon photonics starts with manufacturing. Somebody has to take a silicon wafer and etch photonic circuits onto it — lasers, waveguides, modulators — all at the nanometer scale. That's what foundries do. And in this space, two public companies stand out.



Tower Semiconductor (TSEM)


[EDITOR: show TSEM stock chart + company logo]


Tower Semiconductor is a specialty analog foundry. They don't design chips — they manufacture them. Think of them as the factory that makes the plumbing. And their silicon photonics and RF infrastructure segment just had a breakout year. Revenue in that segment grew 75% in 2025 compared to 2024. That was their fastest-growing business.


[SCREENSHOT: TSEM Q4 2025 earnings highlights — Benzinga]


Now let's talk numbers. Total revenue for 2025 came in at $1.57 billion — up about 9% year over year. They're guiding Q1 2026 revenue to $412 million, plus or minus 5%. And their updated 2028 model is targeting $2.84 billion in revenue. So they're projecting to nearly double the business in three years.


But here's the big move. Tower just committed $920 million in CapEx for 2026 and 2027 — specifically aimed at 5x-ing their silicon photonics and SiGe capacity. And over 70% of that new capacity is already reserved or prepaid by customers.


[EDITOR: show CapEx commitment graphic — $920M, 5x capacity, 70%+ reserved]


That's a huge commitment for a company doing $1.6 billion in revenue. But when 70% of the capacity is already spoken for before you even build it — that tells you something about demand.


And then there's the NVIDIA partnership. This is the foundry NVIDIA chose for their 1.6T optical modules. That's a stamp of approval that matters.


Now the valuation. The stock is trading at a forward P/E around 45. Price-to-sales ratio is about 9.2. That's not cheap. But here's something I like — their balance sheet shows essentially zero long-term debt. For a company making this kind of capital commitment, that's a strong position to be in. Analysts have a consensus target of roughly $157 — and Benchmark just raised their target to $165. At a current price around $130-135, that implies 15 to 25% upside depending on which estimate you use.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com TSEM forecast]


The bear case? That $920 million CapEx is enormous. If AI infrastructure spending slows — or if TSMC enters and commoditizes the manufacturing — Tower could be overbuilt. Customer concentration is a real risk too. But right now, the demand signals are about as strong as you'll find in this space.



GlobalFoundries (GFS)


[EDITOR: show GFS stock chart + company logo]


GlobalFoundries is the third-largest foundry in the world. And most people don't think of them as a silicon photonics play. But they should.


On their Q4 2025 earnings call — literally four days ago — GFS said they doubled silicon photonics revenue within communications infrastructure and data center to over $200 million in 2025. And they expect to nearly double it again in 2026. So we're talking $200 million going to roughly $400 million.


[SCREENSHOT: GFS Q4 2025 earnings transcript quote — Motley Fool]


Now in the context of GFS's total revenue — about $6.8 billion — silicon photonics is still small. Maybe 3% of the total. But it's the fastest-growing segment. And they just expanded to 46,000 wafers per month for SiPho production.


The valuation is actually one of the more attractive you'll find in this space. Forward P/E around 26. PEG ratio of 0.89 — which is solid. They're sitting on $1.8 billion in cash and generating over $1 billion in free cash flow.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com GFS forecast]


The bull case is that GFS gives you silicon photonics exposure on top of a stable, diversified foundry business. You're getting a $200-to-$400 million SiPho growth story inside a company that already generates a billion dollars in free cash flow.


The bear case is that overall revenue growth has been basically flat — up just 0.6% year over year. The base business isn't exciting. You're buying this for the SiPho kicker, not the core. And at 3% of revenue, it's still a small piece of a big company.



The Wild Card: TSMC


Now before we leave the foundry layer — I need to mention TSMC. The 800-pound gorilla. TSMC announced their COUPE platform for co-packaged optics mass production in 2026. And this cuts both ways. TSMC entering silicon photonics manufacturing is a massive validation of the technology. But it's also a competitive threat to Tower Semiconductor and GlobalFoundries. When TSMC enters any market, they tend to drive prices down. That's great for adoption. Not great for smaller foundries trying to maintain margins. This is probably the single biggest risk factor hanging over the foundry layer.



Now — the foundries are only half the story. Somebody has to build the actual optical components that go on top of those chips. The lasers. The transceivers. The parts that actually convert electrical signals into light.



THE COMPONENT MAKERS — Who Builds the Optical Parts


This is the middle layer of the stack. The foundries make the chips. But the component makers build the modules — the transceivers, the lasers, the optical engines that plug into the networking equipment. Without these companies, you've got a silicon chip with no way to move light.



Coherent (COHR)


[EDITOR: show COHR stock chart + company logo]


Coherent — formerly II-VI — is one of the largest optical transceiver makers globally. They make the lasers, the photonic components, and the transceivers that actually convert electrical signals to optical signals. If Tower makes the foundry, Coherent makes the parts that go on top of it.


And their numbers are impressive. Revenue hit a record $1.5 billion in their most recent quarter — up 24% year over year. The full-year revenue run rate is now around $6.3 billion and climbing. And they're seeing acceleration in 800G and 1.6T transceiver demand heading into calendar 2026.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com COHR forecast]


They're also expanding into new product lines. Optical circuit switches for AI data centers. Diamond silicon carbide composites for data center cooling. So it's not a one-trick business.


Now here's where it gets interesting from a valuation standpoint. The trailing P/E is over 200 — which looks scary on the surface. But the forward P/E drops to about 30. And the PEG ratio is just 0.34. That tells you the market expects significant earnings growth ahead — and the price relative to that growth is actually reasonable. Gross margins are around 35% and improving.


The bear case is that Coherent carries about $3.5 billion in long-term debt from their merger with II-VI. And the business is diversified — lasers, materials, industrial products — so you're not getting a pure silicon photonics play. But if the 1.6T transition plays out the way the industry expects, Coherent is sitting right in the middle of it.



Lumentum (LITE)


[EDITOR: show LITE stock chart + company logo]


Lumentum makes the lasers and optical components that go into silicon photonics modules. They're a key supplier of EMLs — externally modulated lasers — and components for 800G and 1.6T transceivers. This is picks and shovels within picks and shovels.


And the growth numbers are strong. Revenue came in at $1.65 billion for fiscal 2025, up 21% year over year. But the revenue run rate is actually accelerating — the trailing twelve months is about $2.1 billion, which is up over 52% compared to the prior period. That's fast.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com LITE forecast]


But here's where I have to be honest with you. The valuation on this one makes me nervous. The forward P/E is about 95. Price-to-sales is over 19. Price-to-book is 47. Those are big numbers. And the free cash flow is still negative — negative $105 million over the trailing twelve months. So you're paying a premium for growth that hasn't fully materialized in the bottom line yet.


The analyst target is around $566, and the stock has already had a massive run — from about $46 at the 52-week low to the $500-600 range. So a lot of the good news may already be priced in.


The bear case is also that their telecom segment — the non-AI business — has been weak. And they carry about $2.6 billion in long-term debt.



So we've got the foundries making the chips. We've got the component makers building the optical parts. Now let's move up the stack to the companies that are designing the actual networking systems — the ones deciding how all these pieces fit together.



THE ARCHITECTS — Who Designs the Systems


These are the companies setting the architecture for how data moves through AI data centers. They're designing the networking chips, the switching platforms, and the protocols that determine which foundries and which components get used. In a lot of ways, this is where the real power sits in the supply chain.



Marvell Technology (MRVL)


[EDITOR: show MRVL stock chart + company logo]


Marvell is a data infrastructure semiconductor company. Networking, storage, custom compute. And they just made a move that I think is under-appreciated.


In December 2025, Marvell announced they're acquiring Celestial AI for over $5 billion. Celestial AI makes something called "Photonic Fabric" — a technology that claims 25x greater bandwidth at 10x lower latency than standard co-packaged optics. They use vertical 3D photonic packaging directly on XPUs.


[SCREENSHOT: Marvell + Celestial AI acquisition press release]


Now $5 billion is a lot of money for a pre-revenue company. But Marvell's CEO said something that stuck with me: "Every connection point in the data center must move from copper to optical." If that's true — and the physics suggests it is — then owning the most advanced photonic interconnect technology could be a significant competitive advantage.


And here's what I like about Marvell's valuation. The forward P/E is about 23. The PEG ratio is 1.5 — reasonable for a growth company. Revenue run rate is $7.8 billion, up 45% year over year. And they're generating $1.4 billion in free cash flow.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com MRVL forecast]


Now I do need to be transparent — Marvell is technically unprofitable on a GAAP basis. Net income is negative due to amortization from prior acquisitions. That's something to keep in mind. But on a cash flow basis, the business is healthy.


The risk is integration. Acquiring a pre-revenue deeptech startup for $5 billion-plus is a big bet. If Celestial AI's photonic fabric doesn't work at scale, that's a lot of money out the door. But if it does — this could be the company that leapfrogs everyone else in the photonic interconnect race.



Broadcom (AVGO)


[EDITOR: show AVGO stock chart + company logo]


Broadcom is the industry leader in co-packaged optics. They just announced their third-generation CPO with 200 Gigabit-per-lane capability. And they've committed to a fourth generation at 400G per lane. They've already shipped over 20 million 100G optical channels and are targeting 5 million 800G units.


[SCREENSHOT: Broadcom CPO announcement — Broadcom IR]


Now here's the thing with Broadcom. Revenue is $63.9 billion. They generated $26.9 billion in free cash flow last year. Gross margins are nearly 68%. This is an absolute machine of a business. Revenue grew 24% year over year — for a company this size, that's remarkable.


The forward P/E is about 32. Analyst target is around $457. And the PEG ratio is 0.9 — actually very reasonable for the quality of this business.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com AVGO forecast]


But silicon photonics is a rounding error in Broadcom's total revenue. You're not buying Broadcom for silicon photonics exposure specifically. You're buying one of the best semiconductor companies in the world — and the co-packaged optics leadership is a bonus.



NVIDIA (NVDA) — The Demand Creator


[EDITOR: show NVDA stock chart + company logo]


Now I'm not including NVIDIA as a silicon photonics "pick" exactly. And here's why. NVIDIA is the demand creator, not the supply chain play. You probably already know whether you own NVIDIA or not.


That said — their commitment to silicon photonics is the single biggest validation of this entire thesis. When Jensen says the future of AI networking runs on co-packaged optics — and then partners with a specific foundry to build it — every company on this list benefits.


[B-ROLL: NVIDIA GTC networking demo footage]


The numbers are insane. Revenue of $130 billion, up 114% year over year. $60 billion in free cash flow. Forward P/E of about 24. For a company growing this fast, that's actually not unreasonable. And the PEG ratio is 0.7.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com NVDA forecast]


But here's the thing — silicon photonics is a strategic initiative for NVIDIA, not a revenue driver. You'd be buying NVIDIA for the GPU business, the AI compute dominance, the networking platform. The SiPho angle is a bonus, not the thesis.



Now — we've covered the foundries, the component makers, and the architects. But there are two more companies that come up in every silicon photonics conversation. And I think it's important you hear about them — because the full picture matters.



THE LEGACY PLAYERS — Who's Trying to Keep Up


These are large, established companies with real history in networking or semiconductors. They have silicon photonics exposure — but they're coming at it from a very different position than the companies we just covered.



Intel (INTC)


[EDITOR: show INTC stock chart + company logo]


Now this one is going to be a little uncomfortable. Because Intel is genuinely a pioneer in silicon photonics. They've been researching this technology since 2004. They demonstrated the first fully integrated optical I/O chiplet in January 2025. They have deep IP in this space.


But — and this is a big but — Intel's patent filings in silicon photonics have plummeted since 2023. They're still at the R&D stage while TSMC is planning mass production. And the company as a whole is struggling. Revenue has declined three years in a row — from $79 billion in 2021 down to $53 billion. Net income was negative $267 million last year. The prior year was a loss of nearly $19 billion.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com INTC forecast]


The stock has had a big bounce recently — from around $18 at the lows to the mid-$50s. But the forward P/E is 91, which is hard to justify when the business is unprofitable and revenue is flat-to-declining.


Look — if Intel Foundry Services gains traction and they can leverage their SiPho IP as a differentiator, there's a story there. But that's a big "if." And the company has much bigger problems to solve first.



Cisco (CSCO)


[EDITOR: show CSCO stock chart + company logo]


Cisco acquired Acacia Communications back in 2021 for about $4.5 billion. Acacia makes silicon photonics-based coherent optics — DSPs and optical modules. And Cisco has been integrating that technology into their routers and switches.


The financials here are actually solid from a business quality standpoint. Revenue is about $57 billion. Forward P/E of 18. They generate over $13 billion in free cash flow. Gross margins are 65%. This is a profitable, stable business.


[SCREENSHOT: StockAnalysis.com CSCO forecast]


But here's the issue — Acacia is a tiny piece of Cisco's overall business. Silicon photonics exposure relative to total revenue is minimal. And Cisco tends to move slowly. They're not the company that's going to lead the 1.6T charge. They'll adopt it — but they won't drive it.



One More Worth Mentioning


STMicroelectronics (STM) — Re-entered silicon photonics in early 2025 with their PIC100 process, built in collaboration with AWS at their 300mm fab in France. The fact that AWS is investing directly in silicon photonics supply chain tells you something about the demand side. But STM isn't really a way to play this theme — SiPho is a small piece of their business.



The Risks — Because There Are Always Risks


Now I wouldn't be doing my job if I didn't talk about what could go wrong. And there are real risks here.


Number one — CapEx overbuilding. Tower is spending $920 million to 5x capacity. GlobalFoundries is expanding. If AI infrastructure spending hits a wall — and we've seen spending cycles slow before — these companies could end up with expensive, underutilized fabs. This is the number one risk I think about.


Number two — TSMC. I mentioned this in the foundry section, but it's worth emphasizing. When TSMC enters any market, they tend to commoditize it. Their COUPE platform in 2026 could drive prices down across the board. Great for hyperscalers buying the technology. Not great for smaller foundries trying to maintain margins.


Number three — timing. The 1.6T ramp could take longer than expected. Standards need to be finalized. Customer qualification cycles are long. Coherent noted that some 1.6T products won't ramp until mid-2026 at the earliest. So if you're expecting immediate revenue acceleration, you might need to be patient.


Number four — valuation. Several of these stocks have already moved significantly. TSEM jumped 12.6% just on the NVIDIA announcement. Lumentum has run from $46 to $600. If you're looking at forward P/Es of 30 to 95 on some of these names — the question is how much growth is already priced in.


Number five — concentration risk. The buyer universe is basically 5 to 7 hyperscalers — AWS, Google, Meta, Microsoft, and a few others. If even one of them delays or cancels a major data center buildout, it ripples through the entire supply chain.


[B-ROLL: stock chart showing recent run-ups]


So please keep in mind — nobody knows exactly how this plays out. I try to be realistic so I can plan ahead. This is not financial advice. Do your own research.

Wrapping It Up


Here's how I think about this. Everyone talks about the AI picks-and-shovels trade — buy the infrastructure instead of trying to pick which AI model wins. And that's smart. But silicon photonics is the picks and shovels within the picks and shovels. It's the plumbing underneath the plumbing.


Every hyperscaler — AWS, Google, Meta, Microsoft — needs this technology. The physics demands it. Copper can't scale. Light can. And 2026 looks like the inflection year.
